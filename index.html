WHAT IS A DUTY DRAWBACK?

WHAT IS A DUTY DRAWBACK? The legal definition for a duty drawback, as found in section 191.2(i) of the U.S. customs regulations is "the refund or remission, in whole or in part, of a customs duty, fee or internal revenue tax which was imposed on imported merchandise under federal law because of its importation, and the refund of internal revenue taxes paid on domestic alcohol as prescribed in 19 U.S.C. 1313(d)" Duty drawbacks are one of the oldest laws in the United States. Passed in 1789, duty drawbacks allow importers under certain conditions to receive a refund of taxes and duties paid upon importing merchandise. When imported merchandise is later exported within three years of importation, a duty drawback may be accessible. The rationale behind this law is to encourage American exports and products overseas as well as to aid American labor and manufacturing. On average, it's estimated that up to 85% of legally available drawbacks aren't claimed and are therefore forfeited to the government. Make sure that your company is not adding to this high statistic by contacting eDRAWBACK today.

Eligibility

Most companies are unaware of their legal rights to request a duty drawback. There are many different situations in which one could be eligible for a duty drawback. Below are the more common scenarios. By law, the drawback must be claimed within three years of exportation of the merchandise. As each day passes, more merchandise passes the three year expiration date. It is, therefore, essential to file the claim promptly. If you think your company is qualified for these rebates please contact us today.

  • Manufacturing direct identification- 19 U.S.C. 1313(a) If your company does manufacturing in the United States with the use of imported material or parts (with the exception of wheat or by products of wheat) and then exports the manufactured product, then your company is eligible for a 99% refund of the import duty paid on the imported material or parts.
  • Manufacturing substitution- 19 U.S.C. 1313(b) If your company manufactures products using both imported and domestic parts of the same kind and quality, even if the exported product doesn't contain the imported parts, but rather the domestic, similar parts, a drawback on the duty of the imported parts can be requested.
  • Unused merchandise direct identification- 19 U.S.C. 1313(j)(1) If your company imports merchandise, and within three years of importation, exports that item or destroys it under customs supervision, a duty drawback of 99% may be requested.
  • Unused merchandise substitution- 19 U.S.C. 1313(j)(2) If your company exports merchandise which is commercially interchangeable with merchandise which was imported within three years before the exportation, a duty drawback is obtainable. (This does not apply if the exportation was to a country included in N.A.F.T.A.)
  • Rejected merchandise- 19 U.S.C. 1313(c) If the merchandise your company received didn't conform to the sample or specifications, or was sent without the consent of the consignee, and was therefore exported or destroyed under customs supervision, a drawback is available. If the merchandise was determined defective upon importation, or was sold to a retailer and then returned to the importer for any reason, and thereupon exported or destroyed under customs supervision within three years of importation, a 99% refund of the importation duty can be requested.
